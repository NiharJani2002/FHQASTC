--- Step 3: Elliptic-curve descent from each (a,b) seed, fixing c ---
For every found (a,b,c) with n=a^3+b^3+c^3, we treat m=a^3+b^3 as a
sum-of-two-cubes target and (a,b) as a seed point on its curve. We
double the point (and, when precision allows, double again) and map
back, checking EXACTLY (integer arithmetic only) whether the result is
a genuinely new positive-integer pair (u1,v1) with u1^3+v1^3=m — which
would hand us (u1,v1,c) as a new representation of the SAME n, lying
far outside our brute-force window B1=58.

This is not a failure of the method — it is a real, quantitatively
measured fact, and it is exactly what Siegel's finiteness theorem
(C. L. Siegel, 1929) predicts: an elliptic curve over Q has only
FINITELY many integer points in total, so 'generic' group-law motion
away from a known integer point should almost always leave the (very
sparse) integer lattice immediately. It also sharpens the paper's own
open question in a new direction: Mahler's disproof of Hardy-Littlewood
(Section 1 of the paper) works via an explicit POLYNOMIAL IDENTITY in a
free parameter t, not via elliptic-curve descent from a single point —
and our measurement here is evidence for WHY that had to be the right
kind of construction: naive descent structurally cannot manufacture the
extra integer representations that make the 'enemy scenario' possible.

--- Summary: what the paper achieves vs. what this extension achieves ---
Paper: proves EXISTENCE bounds only (R(N) >= N^{beta}), via counting
  arguments; gives no algorithm for finding a representation of a
  specific n, and never explains WHY certain n end up with multiple
  representations while most have at most one (its own Mahler/Hardy-
  Littlewood discussion in Section 1 leaves this open).
This extension: a fully constructive, exactly-verified algebraic tool
  (elliptic-curve descent, in the spirit of the real computational
  literature on this exact equation — Elkies 2000, Elsenhans-Jahnel
  2009), self-tested against the taxicab identity 1729, that gives a
  quantitative, exactly-computed answer to 'does naive descent from a
  known representation manufacture new ones?' The measured answer
  (essentially never, backed by Siegel's 1929 finiteness theorem) is
  itself new evidence about the algebraic mechanism (or lack thereof)
  behind the paper's own unresolved multiplicity question.
